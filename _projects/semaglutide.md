**Semaglutide** ([Ozempic/Wegovy](https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2032583)) is among the most effective obesity interventions, yet insurance coverage remains affordability-based, systematically excluding the highest-need patients. This work develops a **necessity-driven risk stratification framework** to replace that model. Published in [*Diabetes*](https://doi.org/10.2337/db25-1050-P) at the [ADA 85th Scientific Sessions](https://professional.diabetes.org/scientific-sessions).

---

## Motivation

Obesity-related chronic conditions (hypertension, prediabetes, hypercholesterolemia) are strongly correlated with BMI progression. The patients most likely to benefit from Semaglutide are often the least able to afford it. Affordability-based coverage criteria are inequitable and actuarially inefficient, covering lower-need patients while the highest-need populations go without access.

---

## Novelty

Using [CDC NHANES survey data (2019-2023)](https://www.cdc.gov/nchs/nhanes/index.htm) across a retrospective cohort design, this study:

- Quantifies correlations between BMI increments and the prevalence of major chronic conditions
- Assigns priority scores to patient subgroups based on correlation strength and associated annual medical costs
- Incorporates an [AI neural network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_neural_network) to model nonlinear relationships between comorbidity predictors and coverage outcomes
- Projects long-term actuarial savings under the necessity-driven model vs. the status quo

---

## Impact

The model projects **$269 billion in 10-year U.S. healthcare savings** while advancing equitable access for high-need populations. First-author publication in [*Diabetes*](https://doi.org/10.2337/db25-1050-P) (American Diabetes Association, 2025). Sole high school presenter at the **[ADA 85th Scientific Sessions](https://professional.diabetes.org/scientific-sessions)**.
